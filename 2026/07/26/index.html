Worth Everything You Have

A buried treasure. A single pearl. Two tiny stories that both end the same way: someone lets go of everything else, and doesn't look back.

Matthew 13:44–52

⚖The Gospel Reading

The kingdom of heaven is like unto a treasure hidden in a field. Which a man having found, hid it, and for joy thereof goeth, and selleth all that he hath, and buyeth that field.

Again the kingdom of heaven is like to a merchant seeking good pearls.

Who when he had found one pearl of great price, went his way, and sold all that he had, and bought it.

Again the kingdom of heaven is like to a net cast into the sea, and gathering together of all kind of fishes.

Which, when it was filled, they drew out, and sitting by the shore, they chose out the good into vessels, but the bad they cast forth.

So shall it be at the end of the world. The angels shall go out, and shall separate the wicked from among the just.

And shall cast them into the furnace of fire: there shall be weeping and gnashing of teeth.

Have ye understood all these things? They say to him: Yes.

He said unto them: Therefore every scribe instructed in the kingdom of heaven, is like to a man that is a householder, who bringeth forth out of his treasure new things and old.

Matthew 13:44–52, Douay–Rheims (Challoner revision)

⚖Reflection

Neither man in these parables sets out to give up everything. The first one is just plowing someone else's field and stumbles onto a treasure he wasn't looking for. The second has been searching for pearls his whole career and finally finds the one that makes every other pearl look ordinary. Different paths, same ending: once they see what they've found, the rest of what they own stops mattering.

Nobody in the story feels like they sacrificed anything. Jesus says twice that the man acts "for joy" — this isn't grim renunciation, it's someone who finally got the math right.

A familiar version of this: a nurse picks up every overtime shift she can get — good money, and it never feels wrong to take it. Then a friend asks her to sit with hospice patients on Wednesday nights, just an hour, just to be present while someone dies without their family in the room.

She goes once. She doesn't go back to her old schedule the same person. The overtime is still available. It's just quietly stopped being the thing she organizes her week around. Nobody told her to choose; she just finally saw what she'd found.

What have you already found that's worth reorganizing your life around — and what are you still holding onto instead?

Closing Prayer

Lord, let me see it when I find it. Don't let me walk past the one thing that matters because my hands are already full of smaller things. Give me the joy those two men had — not the grit of giving something up, but the relief of finally knowing what to hold onto. Amen.
