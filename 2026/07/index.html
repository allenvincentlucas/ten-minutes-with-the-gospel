Feast of Saint Mary Magdalene · July 22

She Knew Him When He Called Her Name

Mary stays at the tomb after everyone else has gone home. She's crying so hard she doesn't recognize the risen Jesus standing right in front of her — until he says one word: her name.

“Jesus saith to her: Mary. She turning, saith to him: Rabboni.” John 20:16, Douay-Rheims
The Gospel Reading

John 20:1-2, 11-18

1And on the first day of the week, Mary Magdalen cometh early, when it was yet dark, unto the sepulchre; and she saw the stone taken away from the sepulchre. 2She ran, therefore, and cometh to Simon Peter, and to the other disciple whom Jesus loved, and saith to them: They have taken away the Lord out of the sepulchre, and we know not where they have laid him.

11But Mary stood at the sepulchre without, weeping. Now as she was weeping, she stooped down, and looked into the sepulchre, 12And she saw two angels in white, sitting, one at the head, and one at the feet, where the body of Jesus had been laid. 13They say to her: Woman, why weepest thou? She saith to them: Because they have taken away my Lord; and I know not where they have laid him. 14When she had thus said, she turned herself back, and saw Jesus standing; and she knew not that it was Jesus. 15Jesus saith to her: Woman, why weepest thou? whom seekest thou? She, thinking it was the gardener, saith to him: Sir, if thou hast taken him hence, tell me where thou hast laid him, and I will take him away. 16Jesus saith to her: Mary. She turning, saith to him: Rabboni (which is to say, Master). 17Jesus saith to her: Do not touch me, for I am not yet ascended to my Father. But go to my brethren, and say to them: I ascend to my Father and to your Father, to my God and your God. 18Mary Magdalen cometh, and telleth the disciples: I have seen the Lord, and these things he said to me.

Reflection

The Sign She Wasn't Looking For

Mary comes to the tomb still grieving and finds it empty, which only makes things worse. Peter and John look, confirm the body is gone, and go home. Mary can't. She stays and cries, looking into the tomb again and again as if looking harder might change what's inside it. Then she turns and sees a man she takes for the gardener — until he says one word: her name. That's the whole turn of the story. Not an argument. Not a proof. A voice that knows her.

A modern version of the same moment

Say you've just lost a job you loved, and the weeks after are a blur of job boards, rejection emails, and well-meaning advice from people who don't really know your situation. None of it lands. Then one evening a friend texts, out of nowhere: “Thinking about you today — how are you actually doing, not the version you tell everyone else?” They use your name. They ask about the real thing. And somehow that lands where three weeks of advice didn't. You don't feel more informed. You feel found.

That's closer to what happens to Mary than any tidy lesson about faith versus doubt. She wasn't lacking information — she'd seen the empty tomb with her own eyes. What she needed was to be known. And the risen Christ doesn't lead with an explanation. He leads with her name.

A question to sit with

Is there a place in your life right now where you're looking for proof, when what you actually need is to be called by name?

A closing prayer:

Lord, when I stand at my own empty places — grieving something, confused by something, certain the story is over — help me stop staring into the dark long enough to hear you say my name. I don't need every answer today. I just need to know you're near, and that you know me. Amen.

Catechism Cross-References

Going Deeper

CCC 640

The empty tomb, on its own, doesn't prove the Resurrection — someone could have moved the body. But it was still an essential sign, the first thread the disciples had to pull on.

Read at vatican.va →
CCC 641

The Catechism names Mary Magdalene and the other women as the very first to encounter the risen Christ, and the first ones entrusted to carry that news to the apostles.

Read at vatican.va →
CCC 2666

The name “Jesus” itself carries his presence — to say it is to invoke him. Which makes it fitting that the turning point in this Gospel isn't a sight, but a spoken name.
