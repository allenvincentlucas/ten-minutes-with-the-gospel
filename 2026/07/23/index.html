Thursday of the Sixteenth Week in Ordinary Time

Blessed Are Your Eyes, Because They See

Jesus doesn't speak in riddles to keep people out — the parable only ever reveals to a heart that's already leaning toward what's being shown.

Matthew 13:10–17

❦The Gospel Reading

And his disciples came and said to him: Why speakest thou to them in parables?

Who answered and said to them: Because to you it is given to know the mysteries of the kingdom of heaven: but to them it is not given. For he that hath, to him shall be given, and he shall abound: but he that hath not, from him shall be taken away that also which he hath. Therefore do I speak to them in parables: because seeing they see not, and hearing they hear not, neither do they understand.

And the prophecy of Isaias is fulfilled in them, who saith: By hearing you shall hear, and shall not understand: and seeing you shall see, and shall not perceive. For the heart of this people is grown gross, and with their ears they have been dull of hearing, and their eyes they have shut: lest at any time they should see with their eyes, and hear with their ears, and understand with their heart, and be converted, and I should heal them.

But blessed are your eyes, because they see, and your ears, because they hear. For, amen, I say to you, many prophets and just men have desired to see the things that you see, and have not seen them, and to hear the things that you hear and have not heard them.

Matthew 13:10–17, Douay–Rheims (Challoner revision)

❦Reflection

The disciples pull Jesus aside and ask the obvious question: why the riddles? His answer isn't about cleverness or IQ. It's about which way a heart is already leaning. A crowd hears a nice little story about a farmer and some seeds. The disciples hear an invitation into how the kingdom actually works. Same words, same voice, same afternoon by the same lake — but a completely different capacity to receive them.

That's the uncomfortable part. Jesus isn't hiding truth from people out of spite or picking favorites at random. He's describing something that's just true about how revelation works: it can only land in a heart that's already turned toward it, even a little. The parable doesn't create that openness. It finds it, or it doesn't.

Somewhere in the middle of an ordinary week: your manager has told you the same thing for three years running — "stop firefighting, build a system" — and every time it went in one ear and out the other, filed under things managers say. Then one Tuesday, mid-crisis, the exact same sentence lands like a thunderclap. Nothing about the sentence changed. You did.

That's not your manager suddenly explaining it better. That's you finally having the ears for it.

This is what makes today's Gospel more hopeful than it first sounds. It isn't a passage about who's in and who's out. It's a passage about readiness — and readiness is something that can grow. The disciples weren't smarter than the crowd; they'd simply spent enough time following Jesus around that something in them had softened toward what he was saying. Blessed eyes aren't a trait you're born with. They're a posture you keep choosing.

What's something God has been saying to you for a while — through a friend, a homily, a nagging thought at 2 a.m. — that you've heard a hundred times but haven't yet let land?

❦Catechism Cross-References

CCC 546–547 The Catechism explains that Jesus' parables aren't decoration on his teaching — they're the characteristic shape it takes, inviting a real decision rather than handing over information for free. Entering the kingdom means becoming a disciple; for those who stay outside that commitment, the same words stay a puzzle. Read the source at the Vatican's official Catechism text.

CCC 153–154 Faith itself, the Catechism says, is a grace that "opens the eyes of the mind" — it's not purely something we work up through better arguments or more effort. And yet believing is still a fully human act, something we freely do, not something merely done to us. Read the source at the Vatican's official Catechism text.

Closing Prayer

Lord, open my eyes to what you've already been showing me, and my ears to what you've already been saying. Don't let familiarity dull what should still astonish me. Give me a heart soft enough to actually receive what you offer, today, in the ordinary words I'll hear a dozen times before I finally let one of them land. Amen.
